Our Work

 

‘Evolve’ is run by a team of UK barristers who previously volunteered for the Centre for Capital Punishment Studies (‘CCPS’) before its closure in 2014.

 

Evolve’s aims are broadly to:

 

(a)   Assist, facilitate and advise on all issues connected to justice and access to justice, particularly in relation to individuals facing or convicted of capital crimes.

(b)  To work with the legal profession, judiciary, non-governmental organisations, private companies, government and other relevant bodies in Uganda on matters relating to efficiency, integrity and fairness in the criminal justice system. 

 

i.                 The ‘Kigula’ Project:

 

In 2009, the Supreme Court of Uganda in Attorney General v Susan Kigula & 417 others (Constitutional Appeal No 3 of 2006) (‘Kigula’) found that the previous mandatory death penalty regime was unconstitutional. For individuals still in the process of appealing their capital convictions, the Supreme Court ruled that their cases should be remitted to the High Court for mitigation and re-sentencing. Those affected by this ruling are known as the ‘Kigula beneficiaries’.

 

The first Kigula cases were listed in the High Court on an ad hoc basis. Approximately 60 [m1] individuals were able to mitigate in this way. Liam Loughlin (Barrister at Church Court Chambers) represented two such individuals in 2012 and secured fixed-term sentences for [MA2] them. However, many remained imprisoned without a lawful sentence – unable to access the Courts and exercise their legal rights. Evolve’s barristers took a leading role in assisting Kigula beneficiaries to go back to the High Court for mitigation and re-sentencing.

 

Most of the remaining Kigula beneficiaries have now been dealt with by way of three special re-sentencing sessions (November 2013, July 2014 and August 2015). We assisted the Kigula beneficiaries, judiciary and members of the legal professions in a number of ways both prior to and during the course of these hearings. The following is a summary of the ways in which we helped. Our aim throughout was to ensure due process, a fair hearing for former death row prisoners and to promote the rule of law.

 

Pre-sentence and social enquiry reports, mental health assessments and prison reports:

 

In preparation for the first special mitigation session in November 2013, Evolve’s founder, Tanya Murshed, worked with the judiciary, the Uganda Law Society, the Ministry of Labour, Gender and Social Development (MGLSD), two psychiatrists and the Uganda Prison Service to pilot the first scheme of independent pre-sentence and social enquiry reports, mental health assessments and prison reports in Uganda. These reports were produced for each person being re-sentenced. The scheme was repeated in each of the subsequent special re-sentencing sessions.  

 

The purpose of the reports was to provide the Court with vital pieces of information about the convict and the circumstances of the offence in order to assist the Court in determining the most appropriate sentence for each convict. 

 

For example, if a mental health assessment revealed that a convict was suffering from a significant mental disorder at the time of the offence or at the time of sentence, according to international law they should not be sentenced to death.

 

Even a single mitigating factor identified in a pre-sentence report could lead to an individual being speared the death sentence. This is because the Court may only pass a sentence of death in exceptional circumstances and in the “rarest of the rare” cases where the alternative of imprisonment for life or other custodial sentence is demonstrably inadequate.

 

Many of the Kigula beneficiaries had been in prison for many years. Prison reports often provided evidence of both the possibility and the reality of rehabilitation. Evidence of a convict’s progress in prison contained in a prison report was decisive because in order for the death penalty to be imposed, there must be no reasonable prospect of the offender’s reform.

 

Training and support:

 

Prior to the November 2013 and July 2014 special re-sentencing sessions, two mitigation seminars were organised by Tanya Murshed and led by Declan O’Callaghan (a barrister at Landmark Chambers). They were attended by a wide range of interested parties, including members of the judiciary and most of the advocates involved in the hearings (defence and state). These seminars covered important principles of international law relating to the death penalty. Further training and support was provided to defence lawyers prior to and throughout the re-sentencing sessions.

 

Drafting of written submissions for defence advocates:

 

Evolve’s barristers drafted written submissions for many of the individuals falling to be re-sentenced. These submissions were used by the defence advocates for oral submissions and were submitted to the sentencing judge. Amicus submissions drafted by Declan O’ Callaghan were also provided in relation to the legal issues affecting the sentencing of the Kigula beneficiaries. These representations covered issues such as the definition of the ‘rarest of the rare’ case, post-conviction mitigation, the relevance of mental illness and learning difficulties at the sentencing stage and the law relating to the sentencing of children in capital cases.

 

Logistical and administrative support:

 

Administrative assistance was sought from and provided by the United Nations Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights who assisted with photocopying each file for all parties involved in the re-sentencing process. Without this support, it is unlikely that the special re-sentencing sessions would have gone ahead.

 

Overseeing the collection of mitigation by law students from Makerere University:

 

The Mitigation Project trained law students from Makerere University in the principles of sentencing and mitigation and interview skills. As the students spoke Luganda as well as other dialects, they were vital to the functioning of the project. They interviewed and collected information relevant to mitigation from every inmate being re-sentenced and from other relevant individuals, such as family members. The information collated in the course of these individuals was given to the defence advocates to assist them in preparing their pleas in mitigation.

This aspect of the project enhanced students’ theoretical legal education with practical experience and provided specialist paralegal skills to students, encouraging them to respect case preparation and the intricacies of criminal procedure and evidence during case observation and file completion. A number of the students have now qualified as lawyers in Uganda and continue to assist the project on a pro bono basis. In this way it is hoped that the project will be self-sustaining on a local level. 

File tracing exercise:

 

A large number of convicts in Uganda have had their files lost or mismanaged. This included Kigula beneficiaries who were consequently unable to exercise their right to have their cases heard by the High Court for mitigation and re-sentencing because their files could not be traced. Following the first special re-sentencing session, authority was obtained to conduct personal searches of the archives at the Supreme Court and Court of Appeal. The student interns played a crucial part in this. Prior to the second special re-sentencing session, 66 files [m3] were located. Almost all of the individuals affected have now been re-sentenced in the July 2014 or August 2015 special re-sentencing sessions.

 

Following the file tracing exercise in the Supreme Court and Court of Appeal, 28 files affecting 32 individuals [m4] remained outstanding. Funding was secured from the International Committee of the Red Cross by Marianne Alton (barrister at Lincoln House Chambers) for a further file tracing exercise to be conducted of the district courts. This exercise was conducted in association with African Prisons Project. Following this file tracing exercise, a further 10 files affecting 15 individuals were located. Where a file could not be located, an official letter was always obtained confirming this. 18 files affecting 23 individuals remain outstanding. 

 

Results:

 

November 2013 special re-sentencing session:

Of 136 individuals re-sentenced during the first special re-sentencing session:

o   8 were released having served sufficient time,

o   92 were given sentences of a term,

o   22 were given life sentence,

o   5 were sentenced to minister’s orders, and

o   9 had their death sentences maintained. 

 

127 individuals were assisted in coming off death row.

 

July 2014 special re-sentencing session:

Of 91 individuals [m5] re-sentenced during the second special re-sentencing session:

o   13 were released having served sufficient time,

o   63 were given sentences of a term, 

o   3 were given life sentences, and

o   13 had their death sentences maintained.

 

79 individuals were assisted in coming off death row.

 

August 2015 special re-sentencing session:

 

Of 9 individuals re-sentenced during the third special re-sentencing session:

o   8 were given sentences of a term,

o   1 had their death sentence maintained.

 

8 individuals were assisted in coming off death row.

 

In all, 214 individuals were assisted in coming off death row.

 

ii.               Capital cause lists:

 

We have been involved in capital cause lists at the Kampala High Court, overseeing the collection of mitigation and preparing submissions for sentencing hearings for those convicted of capital offences. We have also assisted and advised defence advocates on all aspects of trial preparation such as disclosure, admissibility of evidence and cross- examination. Liam Loughlin produced pamphlets on disclosure, hearsay and identification evidence for defence advocates, which have been published on the Uganda Law Society website.

Capital cause list February 2012:

35 cases involving 53 individuals were listed for trial at the Kampala High Court. Liam Loughlin and Amelia Montgomery advised defence advocates throughout this process and oversaw the collection of mitigating evidence for those that were convicted. Mitigation was collected by students from Makerere University who received training. There were 14 convictions (nine were convicted and five pleaded guilty). No sentences of death or life- imprisonment were passed - they were all given fixed-term sentences. In addition, there were three acquittals, 12 nolle prosequi’s, eight cases that were discontinued and two cases were adjourned. One person unfortunately died whilst on bail. [m6] 

Capital cause list June 2013:

In the cause list held between June 2013 - July 2013 which had 19 cases and 28 defendants, there were two nolle prosequi’s, nine acquittals, 13 convictions and four individuals who had their cases adjourned to the next session. Tanya Murshed prepared written submissions for all defendants convicted except one (as he refused to be interviewed). No death or life sentences were passed on any of those convicted. In one case, a police officer had been charged with two counts of murder and four counts of attempted murder for shooting at a mob who were trying to kill suspected thieves. He pleaded guilty to two counts of manslaughter and four counts of unlawful wounding, which was accepted by the prosecution and the judge. Although the maximum sentence for manslaughter is life imprisonment, as a result of mitigation gathered and written submissions provided to the judge, the police officer was given a prison term of four years.

Capital cause list October 2013:

Similarly, in the cause list of October 2013 involving 30 cases and 53 individuals, no sentences of death or life imprisonment were passed. We oversaw the collection of mitigation and provided written submissions for defence advocates for all those that were convicted.

iii.             Appeals against conviction:

 

Although a defendant facing or convicted of a capital offence is entitled to a state brief at every stage of the proceedings, they do not have continuous representation. After a person has been convicted in the High Court, a defence lawyer’s brief comes to an end. As a result, most convicts have to wait many years before their case is listed at the appellate court, as they have no lawyer progressing the matter in the interim. They are only allocated a state brief after a case is listed, who often does not even visit the inmate in prison to take instructions before the appeal. As such, most individuals receive wholly inadequate representation on appeal.

Evolve’s barristers are directly involved in five appeals involving 12 appellants against conviction. All the appellants have been convicted of murder and given the death sentence.

We have also been working with Justice Centres to Uganda, who provide legal assistance to the poor and vulnerable to ensure that all those on death row who have appealed against their conviction have legal assistance.
